When I walked into Family Care Network on my first day, fresh badge in hand and still figuring out where the bathrooms were, I didn't fully grasp what the next few months were going to look like. I knew I'd be doing IT work. I didn't know I'd be crawling through network racks, chasing unlabeled ethernet ports, provisioning somewhere in the neighborhood of three hundred phones, or getting a front row seat to the kind of infrastructure that keeps a multi-clinic healthcare organization running every single day.

The technical growth was real and it was fast. In the span of one quarter I went from watching Ryan configure switches to understanding why the VLAN assignments matter. I went from shadowing help desk calls to independently tracking down missing devices across multiple systems and writing up the findings. I learned that Windows deployment via Intune is slick until the ethernet cable isn't fully seated, that Microsoft will always move the menu right before you need it, and that automation is never not the answer. EPIC, Citrix, VMware, NinjaOne, Meraki, PoE, VLANs, patch panels, ghost cables, and one very mysterious Texas Instruments device hiding behind a wall somewhere at FHA. It's a lot. And somehow it all started to make sense.

But the thing that surprised me most wasn't technical at all. It was the people. Derrick keeping the whole operation grounded and thinking three steps ahead. Ryan pulling back the curtain on sys admin work with patience and genuine enthusiasm. Vir showing me an entire universe of infrastructure I didn't even know existed. Eamon making sure I knew where the fire extinguishers were. The Practice Managers who brought donuts and replied to compliance emails. Every single one of them taught me something.

I came in thinking IT was mostly about fixing computers. I'm leaving knowing it's really about keeping people connected, protecting systems that matter, solving problems that don't always have obvious answers, and doing it all without anyone noticing how much work goes on behind the scenes. Family Care Network gave me more than a resume line. It gave me a real picture of what a career in IT actually looks like, and I genuinely loved every part of it. Even the crazy amount of phone provisioning and installs. Maybe especially those.
